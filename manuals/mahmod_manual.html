2 · Core concepts you must own

Embedding vs referencing

The rule I applied: embed what is owned and read together, reference what is shared and edited independently. Variants live inside Product (they have no life of their own). Addresses live inside User. But Order references User and Product, because an order isn't part of a product's identity and vice-versa.

Snapshots for history

Orders store a copy of each line's product name and price at purchase time; Purchases snapshot the product name and buyer name. If a product is renamed or repriced later, past orders must not silently change — a snapshot freezes the truth.

Denormalization with hooks

Product.ratingAvg and reviewCount are denormalized so a product card never aggregates reviews at read time. A review transaction recomputes them on every create/edit/delete — the write pays the cost so the read is cheap.

Indexes as design, not afterthought

Unique email; unique (user, product) for reviews; unique category slug; a partial unique index on active low-stock alerts; a 180-day TTL index on product events; a text index for search; compound indexes on the audit log. Each one exists to serve a specific query or enforce a specific rule.

Money as integer cents

Every monetary field is an integer count of cents. No Number dollars, ever — floats lose precision and money can't afford that.

3 · End-to-end trace: a review is written

  1. Client posts a review for a delivered product. The controller confirms a delivered Order references that product for this user (verified-purchase rule).
  2. The unique (user, product) index guarantees at most one review per person — a duplicate throws E11000 instead of creating a second.
  3. Inside a transaction, the Review is written and the parent Product's ratingAvg/reviewCount are recomputed, so the two never disagree.
  4. The product card elsewhere reads those denormalized fields directly — no aggregation at request time.

4 · The 14 models — questions & model answers (25+)

Q1 · Why are variants embedded in Product instead of their own collection?
A variant has no meaning without its product and is always read with it. Embedding means one query loads the product and all its variants, with atomic updates. A separate collection would add joins for no benefit.
Q2 · Why are addresses embedded in User?
Addresses are owned by one user and read with the profile. Embedding keeps them together and avoids a lookup for something that's never shared.
Q3 · Why does Order reference Product but snapshot its name and price?
Reference for identity (which product), snapshot for history (what it was called and cost at purchase). Renaming or repricing a product must never rewrite old orders.
Q4 · What's denormalized on Product and why?
ratingAvg and reviewCount. So listing pages don't aggregate the Review collection on every render — the read is O(1) instead of a group-by.
Q5 · How do you keep those denormalized ratings correct?
A transaction on every review create/edit/delete recomputes them from the Review set. The write is slightly heavier so reads stay cheap and consistent.
Q6 · Why a unique index on (user, product) for reviews?
To enforce "one review per person per product" at the database level — not in app code that a race could slip past. A second attempt throws a duplicate-key error.
Q7 · Explain the partial unique index on LowStockAlert.
It's unique on (productId, variantSku) but only where status is 'active'. So a variant can have exactly one active alert yet many historical resolved ones. A plain unique index wrongly blocked the second resolve.
Q8 · What's a TTL index and where do you use one?
A TTL index auto-deletes documents after an age. ProductEvents carry a 180-day TTL so the view stream self-prunes — no cron job to clean it, and the collection can't grow unbounded.
Q9 · How does text search work?
A text index over product name/description backs GET /products?search=. Mongo tokenises and ranks matches, so search doesn't scan every document.
Q10 · Why compound indexes on the audit log?
The log is queried by actor, by action type, and by target entity. Indexes on those fields keep the "show me everything this admin did" query fast even as the log grows append-only.
Q11 · How is the category tree modeled?
Self-referencing: each Category has an optional parent. Two levels in practice, with a unique slug for clean URLs and slug-based filtering.
Q12 · Where do refresh tokens live in the schema?
On User, as SHA-256 hashes — current and previous — never the raw token. That supports rotation and reuse detection without storing anything replayable.
Q13 · What does the Purchase model store and why the snapshots?
Product id + quantity + unit cost, plus snapshots of the product name and the buyer's name. Procurement records must stay readable even if the product or user is later changed or removed.
Q14 · What is weighted-average cost and where does it live?
Product.costPriceCents. When stock is purchased, the cost blends old and new by quantity, computed atomically in the purchase update. It's how margin stays accurate as buy-prices move.
Q15 · Why store money as cents in the schema?
Integers are exact; floating dollars drift. Every price/total/cost field is an integer number of cents.
Q16 · How is one-cart-per-user enforced?
Cart is keyed to the user; there's one active cart document per account. Items reference products and are stock-validated on read.
Q17 · What are the 9 audit action types for?
Each privileged operation — role change, status change, refund_decision, stock_purchase, moderation, etc. — has a type so the log is filterable and every sensitive action is attributable.
Q18 · How does the Order status history work?
The order keeps its current state plus a history of transitions with reasons and timestamps, so fulfillment is auditable end to end.
Q19 · Why is refund an embedded subdoc on Order?
A refund belongs to exactly one order and is always read with it. Embedding keeps the refund amount, id, and reason next to the order they describe.
Q20 · How does the seed script avoid duplicates?
It's idempotent — it upserts known fixtures (three role accounts, products with variants, orders across statuses, reviews, coupons) so re-running it doesn't pile up duplicates.
Q21 · What's the Notification model shape?
Per-user documents with a type, payload, and read flag, powering the unread count and mark-all-read on the bell.
Q22 · How does ProductRecommendation store results?
Per product, a cached top-10 list produced nightly. Reads are pure cache hits; nothing is computed at request time.
Q23 · Why syncIndexes at boot?
It reconciles live indexes with the schema definition — that's how the old full unique index on LowStockAlert was replaced by the partial one without a manual migration.
Q24 · How is bcrypt used and at what cost?
Passwords are bcrypt-hashed at cost 12 before save via a pre-save hook, so a plaintext password never touches the database.
Q25 · Do you use transactions anywhere?
Yes — the review flow wraps the Review write and the Product rating recompute in a transaction so they commit together or not at all.
Q26 · How many collections total, and what changed recently?
14. The newest is Purchase (procurement/costing); it took the count from 13 to 14 and added the stock_purchase audit type.

5 · Curveballs (10)

C1 · A product has 500 variants. Is embedding still right?
At that scale the 16 MB document limit and per-update rewrite cost start to hurt. For a few dozen variants embedding is ideal; for hundreds I'd move variants to their own collection referenced by product. It's a threshold decision, not a religion.
C2 · Two admins resolve the same low-stock alert simultaneously. What happens?
Both try to flip status to resolved; the update is idempotent (resolving a resolved alert is a no-op) and the partial index only constrains active docs, so there's no duplicate-key clash. Worst case one write is a harmless no-op.
C3 · Your denormalized ratingAvg drifts from the actual reviews. How?
Only if a review write committed but the recompute didn't — which the transaction prevents. If it ever happened (e.g. a manual DB edit), I'd run a reconciliation aggregation to recompute from source. The source of truth is always the Review collection.
C4 · The TTL index deleted view events a report still needed. Now what?
TTL is for ephemeral signal, not records of value. If we needed longer history I'd raise the TTL or roll events up into a durable daily-aggregate collection before they expire. The recommender only needs recent behaviour, so 180 days is deliberate.
C5 · Why not just compute ratings on the fly with an aggregation?
Because product listings are the hottest read path — aggregating reviews for every card on every page load doesn't scale. Denormalization moves that cost to the rare write. Classic read-heavy tradeoff.
C6 · A unique index build fails on production data with duplicates. How do you recover?
The build errors and the index isn't created. I'd find the duplicates with an aggregation group-by, resolve them (merge/remove), then let syncIndexes build it. Never force-drop data to make an index — fix the data first.
C7 · Snapshots duplicate data. Isn't that a normalization smell?
It's intentional denormalization for temporal correctness. Normalization optimises for a single source of truth now; orders need the truth as it was then. Those are different requirements, and history wins here.
C8 · Weighted-average cost vs FIFO/LIFO — why average?
Average cost is simplest to compute atomically in one document update and is defensible for a store like this. FIFO/LIFO need per-lot tracking (separate cost layers), which is a bigger model. Average was the right complexity for the requirement.
C9 · How would you shard this if it got huge?
Product and Order are the growth collections. I'd shard Order by a hashed customer or time-based key and keep Product replicated/small. Reviews shard with their product. The embedded design actually helps — fewer cross-shard joins.
C10 · Mongoose vs the native driver — why the abstraction?
Mongoose gives schema validation, hooks (bcrypt, rating recompute), and populate for referenced docs, which enforce our invariants centrally. The cost is some overhead and magic; for a schema-rich domain like this the safety is worth it.

6 · A new concept I learned

Partial indexes. I used to think a unique constraint was all-or-nothing across a collection. Learning that a unique index can apply to a filtered subset — unique only where status:'active' — solved a real bug elegantly: one active alert per variant, unlimited resolved history, enforced by the database itself. It reframed indexes for me as expressive business rules, not just lookup accelerators.

7 · Still challenging / honest gaps

  • Sharding & scale. I can reason about a sharding strategy but haven't run this under real horizontal load to validate the key choices.
  • Migration discipline. syncIndexes handles index drift, but there's no formal data-migration framework for schema changes on live data.
  • Cost accounting depth. Weighted-average is solid; a full FIFO/LIFO lot-tracking model is a design I understand but haven't built.
