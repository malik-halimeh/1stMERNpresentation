Your learned & challenging

Learned (confident now): Compensating transactions. "MongoDB gives single-document atomicity, but the purchase flow touches two documents. I learned to make the second write reversible and undo the first on failure — that pattern made our costing feature safe without heavyweight machinery."
Still challenging (own it lightly): Designing for horizontal scale. "I can reason about Redis-backed limiting and a cron leader-lock, but I haven't run this across multiple instances for real — that's my next step."

Your top-10 questions

1 · Why two tokens?
Short access token limits theft blast-radius; long rotating refresh keeps users in and can be revoked. One token can't be both safe and convenient.
2 · Why is the access token in memory?
localStorage is script-readable (XSS). Memory dies on reload; we refresh from the http-only cookie.
3 · What is reuse detection?
An old refresh token replayed outside the 30s grace = theft signal → revoke all sessions for that user.
4 · Why raw body before express.json for the webhook?
Stripe signs raw bytes; parse first and the signature can't be verified. It's fail-closed — invalid = 400.
5 · How do you prevent overselling?
Conditional $inc guarded by stock ≥ qty; the DB arbitrates the race, loser clamps to 0.
6 · Why re-validate the coupon at checkout?
The preview is client-controlled; anyone can POST a code straight to checkout, so the server re-checks caps/expiry/min.
7 · How do refunds work?
stripe.refunds.create before persisting, refund subdoc + refund_decision audit, 502 on failure.
8 · How does role change take effect?
Up to 15 min — the role rides in the access token. Accepted cost of stateless authz.
9 · What breaks at multi-instance?
In-memory rate limit splits and cron double-runs; fix = Redis store + leader-lock. Orders/payments already DB-atomic.
10 · How do you know fixes didn't break things?
test_fixes.ts 20/20 + clean builds + HTTP smoke test through the real middleware.

Your learned & challenging

Learned (confident now): Single-flight request coordination. "I used to let each 401 trigger its own refresh. Serialising them behind one shared promise — N failures, one refresh, N replays — changed how I think about async UI: the fix was one point of coordination, not more state."
Still challenging (own it lightly): Automated frontend testing. "We have a backend regression suite; I lean on manual testing for the UI and want real component/interaction tests next."

Your top-10 questions

1 · Why Context instead of Redux?
Three bounded slices (auth, shop, toast) fit Context cleanly without Redux boilerplate.
2 · Where's the access token stored?
In memory in AuthContext — never localStorage — refreshed silently from the http-only cookie on reload.
3 · What is silent refresh?
On a 401, the Axios interceptor refreshes and replays the request; the user sees nothing.
4 · Ten 401s at once — ten refreshes?
No — single-flight: one shared promise, one refresh, the rest replay.
5 · How do guest carts survive login?
localStorage cart merges into the server cart on login; stock re-validated.
6 · Are client route guards secure?
No — UX only. The server authorises every request, so bypassing the guard just yields a 403.
7 · Why is search in the URL?
Shareable, bookmarkable, back-button-correct; the list reads the query param.
8 · How is the design consistent?
Tailwind tokens defined once; components consume tokens, so a theme change is one edit.
9 · Is it responsive?
Mobile-first; header→drawer, admin sidebar collapses, grids reflow.
10 · Why is the bundle 546 kB / what next?
One chunk today; I'd route-split the admin bundle from the storefront with lazy imports.

Your learned & challenging

Learned (confident now): Partial indexes. "I thought a unique constraint was all-or-nothing. A unique index on a filtered subset — unique only where status is active — solved a real bug: one active low-stock alert per variant, unlimited resolved history, enforced by the database. I now see indexes as business rules, not just speed."
Still challenging (own it lightly): A formal data-migration workflow. "Index sync we handle at boot; safely evolving live data through schema changes is the discipline I want to master next."

Your top-10 questions

1 · When embed vs reference?
Embed what's owned and read together (variants, addresses); reference what's shared and edited independently (orders→products).
2 · Why snapshot order name/price?
So renaming/repricing a product never rewrites past orders — history must be immutable.
3 · What's denormalized and why?
ratingAvg/reviewCount on products, so listings read O(1) instead of aggregating reviews.
4 · How stay correct?
A transaction recomputes ratings on every review change — write pays, read stays consistent.
5 · Explain the partial unique index.
Unique on (productId, variantSku) only where status is active — one active alert per variant, unlimited history.
6 · What's a TTL index for?
Product view events auto-delete after 180 days — self-pruning, no cleanup job.
7 · How does search work at the DB?
A text index over name/description ranks matches instead of scanning.
8 · Why money in cents?
Integers are exact; floats drift. Every price/total/cost is integer cents.
9 · Explain weighted-average costing.
New cost = (oldStock·oldCost + qty·newCost)/(oldStock+qty), computed atomically in the purchase update.
10 · Where would the schema strain at scale?
Order and ProductEvent growth; shard Order by customer/time, roll events into daily aggregates. Embedding reduces cross-shard joins.
